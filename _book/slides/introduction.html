Inference duality of observer: Induction, least fixpoint (\(\mu\))

Inference duality is algorithmic mechanism of the observer. Logical Benders decomposition shows how the observer iteratively proposes a model of itself and verifies it.

  • The Master (The Proposer): The observer proposes a relaxed model of its current state or plan.
  • The Subproblem (The Verifier): The observer reflects on this proposal, testing its logical consistency with respect to the overall problem.
  • The Cut (The Inference): When the proposal fails, the observer generates a logical inference (a Benders cut) that restricts its future reasoning.

Hooker & Ottosson, 2003 Logic-based benders decomposition

Davies, Pearce, Stuckey & Lipovetzky 2015, Sequencing Operator Counts, International Conference on Planning and Scheduling (ICAPS) (Best paper award)

  • Shown to be competivie with state of the art planners on International Planning Competition (IPC) benchmarks
  • The Master is a mixed integer program (MIP), which does operator counting.
  • The subproblem is a SAT solver, which verifies if the operators can be sequenced
  • Proven to converge to the optimal feasible solution
  • It can be modeled as finding a fixpoint of a monotonic constraint-generation operator

Limitations:

  • It does not compute a “least fixpoint” in the traditional sense as it intentionally stops early once optimal feasibility is proven (it is based on propositional logic)
  • Note that the situation calculus does rely on least fixpoint in first order logic

Logical interval invariant?

In synthetic relativity, inductive structure and coinductive process are two mathematically intertwined ways of evaluating the same invariant truth.

  • Induction (static space/structure) and
  • Coinduction (unfolding time/process) are the coordinate axes, and
  • Realisation is the invariant interval.

Rotating your logical frame of reference changes how much of the proof exists statically in spatial topology versus how much unfolds dynamically in computational time, but the invariant interval—the Realisation—remains perfectly preserved.

Confluence (or the Church-Rosser theorem) states that if you have a valid computation, the path, perspective, or order in which you evaluate it does not change its final semantic truth.

The logical observer

Different logical observers evaluate systems differently:

  • One observer (evaluating lazily/coinductively) stretches out the “time” of the proof into an infinite behavioral stream.
  • Another observer (evaluating eagerly/inductively) compresses the proof into a massive, static memory structure in “space”.

However, the fundamental computational truth—the Realisation—cannot change.

If truth were not invariant under these frame-shifts, logic would collapse into inconsistency. The spatial distortion of the data structure and the temporal delay in evaluation mathematically cancel out to preserve the invariant Realisation.

Axiomatic transformations in logic

An example in logic is the space-time tradeoff Memoisation versus lazy evaluation.

Memoization (Time \(\rightarrow\) Space)

You take a dynamic, temporal proof that requires many coinductive steps (Time), evaluate it in advance, and cache all the valid answers into a giant, static lookup table (Space/Induction). You have contracted logical time and dilated logical space.

Lazy Evaluation (Space \(\rightarrow\) Time)

You take a massive, eagerly-evaluated structural memory footprint (Space/Induction) and collapse it. Instead of storing the data, you store a tiny coalgebric rule that generates the proofs sequentially, only exactly when requested (Time/Coinduction). You have contracted logical space and dilated logical time.

Autonomous theory progression & modal acceleration via \(\mathbf{GLB}\)

Modal acceleration captures the provability of theory \(A\) relative to theory \(B\) relative to theory \(C\). A transition between modalities.

If we want an agent that doesn’t just search blindly, but accelerates its learning by reflecting on its own provability bounds, we traditionally turn to bimodal provability logics like GLB. GLB gives us the mathematics of modal acceleration and reflection.

  • Autonomous Theory Progression: Polymodal provability logic (\(\mathbf{GLB}\)) indexes a system’s ascent through levels of incompleteness.
  • The Inductive Trap: The de Jongh-Sambin theorem guarantees a unique fixpoint in these spaces.
  • The Collapse: \(\mathbf{GLB}\) forces a well-founded, purely inductive view of reflection.
  • The Problem: A logical observer is an active, continuous process. Classical logic collapses induction (proof generation) and coinduction (invariant state) into a single point.

We can use GLB to model the observer generating logical inferences or Benders cuts inductively.

For this reflection to be rigorous, the syntax (the inference) must perfectly dualize to the semantics (the topology). This is where the limit of standard binary logic is met.

Limitations

  • GLB relies on Boolean logic and strictly well-founded frames (enforced by Löb’s axiom (\(\Box(\Box p \to p) \to \Box p\)), which dualize to Hausdorff spaces. It assumes an absolute, discrete separation of states and guaranteed termination.

  • Standard binary logic (Hausdorff spaces) cannot handle the topology of an observer reflecting upon itself, we lose the coinductive reality of the observer.

  • When our system acts as a continuous computational process, standard Hausdorff spaces and strict binary logic fail.

  • When dealing with continuous computation or fixpoint semantics, the strict, separable \(T_2\) (Hausdorff) spaces of standard Stone duality begin to fail.

  • The space becomes non-Hausdorff. The strict “true/false” binary is structurally insufficient to map self-reflective reasoning.

Categorical (Stone) duality: Domain Theory in Logical Form (DTLF) & Scott topologies

Through Stone duality, DTLF proves that these two sides are mathematically isomorphic:

  • The Spatial Side (Semantics): Domains of computational values equipped with the Scott topology.

  • The Algebraic Side (Syntax): The logic of observable properties, structured as an algebraic lattice.

To capture the continuous reality of the logical observer, we must transition to the semantics of DTLF.

  • Non-Hausdorff Semantics: DTLF provides the formal generalized topological space (e.g., Scott topologies) required for continuous computation.
  • Untangling Fixpoints: DTLF formally provides the architecture to distinguish between least fixpoints (induction) and greatest fixpoints (coinduction).
  • The Implication: Standard binary logic is structurally insufficient for an observer reflecting upon itself.

DTLF is the proof that the crisis is real. It gives us the continuous semantics we need, proving that to maintain a rigorous categorical duality for our observer, we must leave standard discrete spaces behind.

  • DTLF demonstrates how Stone Duality must be generalized (into non-Hausdorff spaces and Scott topologies) to handle continuous computation and fixpoints.

Tri-modality and ternary representation

Because the categorical dual of a reflective system cannot be handled in a strict boolean space without losing information, a ternary representation becomes geometrically and logically necessary.

Standard GLB uses absolute binary provability: \(x R y\) assumes an absolute background space where \(y\) is provable from \(x\).

  • In a ternary structure (drawing from Routley-Meyer semantics for substructural, relevance, and intuitionistic logics), you have a relation \(R(x, y, z)\).
  • This allows you to decouple the acceleration jump from the topological space. The ternary relation introduces the Observer’s frame. It reads: “From the reference frame \(x\), applying the acceleration/Benders cut \(y\) yields the state \(z\).”

If the semantic space is no longer strict and binary, the representational geometry must evolve to match.

  • The Tri-modal Shift: Introducing a ternary representation to dynamically navigate the relativistic observer.
  • Structural Necessity: Tri-modality is the specific geometry required to house the non-standard continuous spaces of DTLF.
  • The Outcome: The observer can safely maintain its operational identity (coinduction) while formally stepping outside its previous logical bounds (induction).

This is the core of Synthetic Relativity. Tri-modality is not an arbitrary choice; it is a mathematical necessity. By moving to a ternary space, the framework successfully untangles the fixpoints, laying the formal foundation for a system that can ultimately achieve self-transcending reasoning.

Synthetic Relativity Axioms

Dynamic Trimodal Logic with Ternary Relational Semantics

The R-DTLF framework replaces absolute global topologies with a relativistic reasoning architecture governed by three interacting modalities:

  • \(\mathrm{RDo}\) (The Subproblem / Syntax): The Inductive Accumulator (\(\Box_1\)) Operates in constructive, non-Boolean logic. Recursively gathers finite, computationally observable properties (compact elements) along a directed logical path.

  • \(\mathrm{ODo}\) (The Master Problem / Semantics): The Limit/Creator Modality (\(\Box_2\)) The semantic evaluator and interval invariant. Performs topological closure—evaluating the directed set generated by \(\mathrm{RDo}\) and snapping it into a discrete, continuous limit.

  • \(\mathrm{PDo}\) (Autonomous Progression): The Relativistic Engine (\(\Box_3\)) The dynamic transition operator. It orchestrates the recursive interaction of local syntax (\(\mathrm{RDo}\)) and global semantics (\(\mathrm{ODo}\)) relative to a specific baseline Reference Frame (\(S_r\)).

Dynamic Unrolling & Realisation

Computing the Interval Invariant

1. The Dynamic Trace (Relative Coordinates) Within a given Reference Frame (\(S_r\)), the Relativistic Engine (\(\mathrm{PDo}\)) iteratively drives the state forward, nesting inductive syntax (\(\mathrm{RDo}\)) and semantic evaluations (\(\mathrm{ODo}\)): \[ \mathrm{PDo}(S_n, S_o, S_r) \] \[ \mathrm{PDo}\big(\, \mathrm{RDo}(R_1, S_n),\; \mathrm{ODo}(O_1, S_o),\; S_r \,\big) \] \[ \mathrm{PDo}\Big(\, \mathrm{RDo}\big(R_2, \mathrm{RDo}(R_1,S_n)\big),\; \mathrm{ODo}\big(O_2, \mathrm{ODo}(O_1,S_o)\big),\; S_r \,\Big) \]

2. The Relative Fixpoint Axiom (Realisation) Autonomous progression strictly halts when the topology reaches Algebraic Compactness. This is the moment of mathematical Realisation: \[ \mathrm{Fix}(\mathrm{PDo}) = \mathrm{ODo} \left( \bigsqcup_{i \in R} \mathrm{RDo}_i(\bot) \right) \]

The ultimate stable fixpoint is caught exactly when \(\mathrm{ODo}\) evaluates the limit (Supremum, \(\bigsqcup\)) of iteratively applying the recursively enumerable inductive proofs (\(\mathrm{RDo}\)) from baseline ignorance (\(\bot\)). This Realised limit acts as the absolute semantic invariant.

The Synthetic Transformation

Transitivity and Change of Base

\[ \mathrm{PDo}(N, O, S_r) \longrightarrow \mathrm{PDo}(X, Y, N) \]
